Bluefin Tuna Fishing Regulations

In the second half of the 20th century, the bluefin tuna started gaining a lot of popularity due to its exceptional food quality, especially in Japan. This rapid increase in demand for the fish led to a lot of over fishing especially of the Atlantic species. According to the ICUN, the Atlantic bluefin tuna is considered an endangered species. To make sure the bluefin tuna stocks increase to safe levels again, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) has put many regulations in place around fishing for the species in the United States. These regulations can differ based on the type of fishing license you have.

Licenses

There are three types of licenses you can buy for bluefin tuna fishing. These licenses are provided by NOAA and are different from a normal recreational fishing license that you buy from the state fish and wildlife department. The licenses provided by NOAA for tuna fishing are called Highly Migratory Species (HMS) permits, and they cover fishing for different species of tuna as well as billfishes and sharks. These permits can be bought at NOAA's permit shop. The three main licenses for bluefin tuna fishing are the angling category, the charter/headboat category, and the general category. The angling category license is for recreational fishermen. The charter/headboat category license is for fishing charter captains. The general category license is for commercial fishermen. All of the licenses cost $26.00. If this is your first time fishing for bluefin tuna, you should definitely purchase the HMS angling category license.

Bag Limits

Depending on the license, there are different rules for the amount of fish you are allowed to keep per day as well as he size of the fish you keep. Keeping more fish than allowed or the wrong sized fish can result in fines of up to $10,000 issued by NOAA. For the HMS angling category license, you are allowed to keep 2 bluefin tuna per day as long as the fish are between 27" and 47". You are only allowed to keep one fish per day if it is between 47" and 73". Any fish that is less than 27" or greater than 73" is not allowed to be kept and must be released with this license. For the HMS general category license, only one fish can be kept per day which must be at least 73" in length. For the HMS charter/headboat category license, the charter captain has the option to choose to follow the rules of the angling category or general category on a given day and follow the rules of that category only. Measuring the length of tunas is different from many other species. Tunas must be measured from the tip of the jaw to the fork in the tail. The curve of the tuna's body is included in this measurement.

Quotas

For commercial fishing, the bluefin tuna season is dictated by quotas set by NOAA. Every year, the bluefin tuna season opens on June 1, and will eventually close once a ceertain cumulative weight of bluefin tuna have been caught and sold. This initial quota is usually reached by some time in the middle of August. NOAA will often then set new single month quotas for September through December. To read news updates about these quotas and other bluefin tuna fishing regulations, visit the News page on the HMS permit shop website.

Catch Reporting

If you are fishing for bluefin tuna, whether that be recreationally or comercially, NOAA requires that you report evry fish you catch within 24 hours of catching the fish. You can report your catch at NOAA's catch reporting page. Information such as the size of the fish and where it was caught must be reported. This is one of the ways NOAA collects data in order to manages the fishery and implement new policies and regulations.
