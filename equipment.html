Equipment

The amount of equipment required to successfully fish for bluefin tuna can be overwhelming. This page will try and break down all the different types of gear, both boating related and fishing related, in the easiest way possible. For boating equipment, this page will stick to aspects of the boat that pertain to this type of fishing and will avoid covering basic boating safety equipment and the likes.

Boating Equipment

The Boat

The boat is obviously the most important piece to the puzzle when it comes to fishing offshore for anything. If you are lucky enough to live on the north or south shores of Massachusetts, it isn't a far ride out to where the tuna are and a very large boat isn't always necessary. A small to medium sized center console or walk around stye boat is often a big enough boat to safely make it out to Stellwagen Bank and back. These boats are also perfect for recreational bluefin tuna fishing, and many saltwater fishermen have these types of boats to begin with. With a boat smaller than this, you may be limited in the days you can fish based on the weather and how windy it is. It is important to make some simple modifications and upgrades to your boat if you want to be a successful bluefin tuna fisherman.

Livewell

A livewell is a small tank that pumps fresh seawater in and out with the purpose of keeping small fish alive to be used as bait. This is an important aspect of tuna fishing when it comes to live bait fishing. Most center console boats have livewells built in already, however small portable livewells are simple to construct on your own if your boat does not have one.

Navigation and Sonar

When most fishermen buy a boat, they will outfit with marine electronics. A navigation unit and sonar unit are extremely important when fishing and often times can be bought on one machine. The navigation unit, or chart plotter is an extremely important tool while fishing. The chart plotter will tell you the depths of the ocean over large areas, as well as show contour lines of the bottom. In addition, you can mark and save locations on the chart itself, so when you find or know of a good spot you will know exactly where it is and be able to drive right up to it. The sonar, also called a fish finder, is another very important tool while fishing. The fish finder is basically underwater radar and will show you in real time what is underneath your boat. This will provide a lot of information such as where the bait is and what depth the tuna are at as they swim by. It is also important when trying to fish on the edge of structure such as Stellwagen Bank so you can make sure you are positioned correctly on the slobe of the bank.

The Anchor

Anchoring is a big aspect of fishing for bluefin tuna in the northeast. Around Cape Cod and especially on Stellwagen Bank, anchoring up and using what is referred to as a "ball" is one of the most popular ways to fish. For this, you will need the actual anchor, which most likely comes with the boat, at least 600 feet of rope, about 100 feet of floating rope, and an anchor ball with a retrieving system. The purpose of the retrieving system is to be able to easily get the anchor back up to the boat without pulling it in by hand. The Techniques page will cover how to set up with the anchor and use the retrieving system.

Rod Holders

While bluefin tuna fishing, leaving the rod in the rod holder is the most popular way to fish. In order to do this successfully, you must have swivel base rod holders on your boat. A swivel base rod holder allows the rod to freely rotate back and forth in the holder as the fish is swimming around. Most boats do not come with these specialty rod holders installed so you may need to replace the stock rod holders or drill new holes in the gunwhale of the boat to istall them. There are a few different companies that make these rod holders including Lee's Tackle, L.T. Marine, and Hooker Electric.

Fishing Equipment

Reels

The fishing reels used for bluefin tuna fishing are conventional reels in the 80 and 130 class. (An 80 class reel means the reel is rated to hold about 1,000 yards of 80 pound test monofilament line.) These reels are used because of their large line capacity and heavy drags. If you are just starting out in bluefin fishing, an 80 class reel will be sufficient. There are a few major brands that make these reels and all of them are very similar in performance as well as price, so it comes down to personal preference when choosing which one to buy. The Tiagra 80 wide reel made by Shimano is probably the most popular on the market. Other companies like Penn and Okuma make very similar reels as well. These reels can last a lifetime if taken care of properly. You can easily find used reels in great condition on Ebay for a much lower price. Now that we have covered the reel, we have to fill it with fishing line. The goal with this type of fishing is to use fishing line with a high breaking strength and still be able to fit a lot of line on the reel. To accomplish this, 130lb hollow core braided fishing line is used. About 2/3 of the reel is filled with this, and the rest of the reel is filled with a topshot of 150-200lb monofilament. It is very important to pack the line on the reel extremely tight so it won't cut into itself when heavy tension is applied. If you take your reel to a local tackle shop, they should be able to spool it for you with the line you want.

The Rod

The fishing rods used for bluefin tuna fishing are very unique to this type of fishing and are often custom built. These rods are built on 7 or 8 foot 130 class blanks with bent butts and large roller guides. The reason the rod has a bent butt is because it will be fished out of the swivel base rod holders on the boat, so the rod will always be pointed at an angle rather than stright up and down. The rod must have the large roller guides in order to reduce friction on the line and be able to fit terminal gear such as swivels and rubber bands through the guides. One company that makes great fishing rods is Reel Easy Custom Rods. Some local big game tackle shops will build these types of rods as well. From a small to medium sized boat, it is hard to fish more than 2 rods at once, so buying to rods and reels to go with them will be good.

Terminal Gear

The subject of terminal gear is probably the most personal and unique when it comes to tuna anglers. Terminal gear includes the hooks, leader, swivels and crimps. For the hooks, many people use large 9/0 or 10/0 circle hooks. Circle hooks are a great choice for recreational anglers because the fish will almost always get hooked in the corner of the mouth and will never be gut hooked. For leaders, flourocarbon line is a must and there are two main reasons for this. While monofilament line will reflect light underwater, flourocarbon is almost invisible in the water so fish will not be able to see it as well. Also, flourocarbon is more abrasion resistant than monofilament so the tuna's small teeth will not e able to bite through it. Using a relatively light pound test leader (150-180lb) is common to reduce the likelyhood of fish being able to see the line. Lastly, you will need swivels and crimps to attach the flourocarbon leader to your main line and your hook to your leader. Before making your leaders, it is a good idea to color all of the swivels, crimps and hooks with a black sharpie so they don't shine underwater.

Miscellaneous

Other fishing gear you will need includes large bank sinkers, different colored balloons (bio degradable), and rubber bands.
